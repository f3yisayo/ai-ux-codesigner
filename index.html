The Prompt Library

Curated prompts for each phase of UX design. Click a card to expand the full prompt — copy and adapt for your context.

Discovery · Research
User Interview Question Generator
You are a UX researcher. I'm designing [PRODUCT] for [USER GROUP]. Generate 10 open-ended interview questions that uncover their current behavior, frustrations, and latent needs around [TOPIC]. Avoid leading questions. Group by theme.
Why it works: Specifies role, product, and user to get contextual questions rather than generic ones.
Synthesis · Personas
Persona from Raw Notes
Here are raw notes from 6 user interviews: [PASTE NOTES]. Identify 2–3 distinct user archetypes. For each, give: name, key goal, primary frustration, mental model, and one verbatim-style quote that captures their mindset. Format as a concise persona card.
Why it works: Grounds AI output in real data, making personas evidence-based rather than invented.
Ideation · Concepts
10x10 Concept Sprints
Problem: [PROBLEM STATEMENT]. Generate 10 radically different design concepts — ranging from obvious to absurd. For each: a catchy concept name, 1-sentence description, and the core UX principle it relies on. Don't filter for feasibility yet.
Why it works: Asking for "absurd" concepts breaks the AI out of safe, generic suggestions.
Information Architecture
IA from User Goals
Users of [APP] need to accomplish these tasks: [LIST TASKS]. Create a site map / navigation structure that groups content by user mental model, not by company org chart. Use a tree format. Flag any items that create cognitive load due to ambiguous placement.
Why it works: Instructs AI to think from user perspective, not business structure.
Copywriting · UX Writing
Microcopy for Moments of Friction
Write UX copy for these 5 friction moments in [APP]: [LIST MOMENTS, e.g. empty state, error message, confirmation dialog, onboarding step, destructive action]. Voice: [ADJECTIVES]. Each should be under 20 words, avoid jargon, and reduce anxiety without hiding risk.
Why it works: Constrains length and specifies emotional tone — key for good microcopy.
Critique · Review
Design Critique Partner
You are a senior UX designer doing a critique. Here is my design rationale for [FEATURE]: [DESCRIPTION]. Challenge my assumptions. What are the 3 biggest risks? What user need might I be ignoring? What cognitive biases might have influenced my decisions? Be direct.
Why it works: Asking for "challenge" and naming biases explicitly pushes AI to give genuinely critical feedback.
Accessibility · Inclusion
Inclusive Design Audit
Review this user flow for [TASK]: [DESCRIBE FLOW]. Identify barriers for users who are: (1) screen reader dependent, (2) have motor impairments, (3) are low-literacy, (4) have cognitive differences, (5) are non-native speakers. Suggest one concrete fix per barrier.
Why it works: Specificity of impairment categories produces actionable, not generic, accessibility notes.
Usability Testing
Test Script Generator
Create a moderated usability test script for [FEATURE]. Include: participant intro & warm-up (5 min), 3 scenario-based tasks with think-aloud prompts, follow-up questions for each task, and a closing debrief. Assume a 45-minute session. Note what behaviors to observe per task.
Why it works: Timing and behavior-observation prompts make the output immediately usable.
Prototyping
Component Spec Writer
Write a detailed component specification for a [COMPONENT NAME] in a [PRODUCT TYPE]. Include: purpose, anatomy (all sub-elements), states (default, hover, focus, disabled, error), interaction behavior, accessibility requirements, and do/don't usage examples. Format for a design system doc.
Why it works: Component spec format is directly useful for handoff to engineering and design system teams.
UX Concepts Explained

Key UX concepts — and exactly how to use AI to explore, apply, or challenge each one.

Mental Models
Research
Users understand your interface through their existing knowledge. When your UI matches their mental model, it feels intuitive. When it doesn't, confusion ensues. AI can help you surface and map users' existing mental models before you design.
→ "Act as a [USER TYPE]. Walk me through, step by step, how you'd expect to [TASK] using an app. Use present tense. Don't describe any specific app — describe what you'd naturally expect."
Jobs To Be Done
Research
People don't buy products — they hire them to do a job. JTBD reframes UX around functional, social, and emotional jobs rather than features. AI is powerful for generating and stress-testing job statements.
→ "Turn this user story into a JTBD format: 'When [SITUATION], I want to [MOTIVATION], so I can [EXPECTED OUTCOME].' Then write 3 variants that represent different underlying jobs."
Cognitive Load
Ideation
Working memory is limited. Every decision, option, and piece of information in your UI adds load. Reducing intrinsic, extraneous, and germane cognitive load is a core UX principle AI can help audit for.
→ "Here's a form / screen description: [DESCRIPTION]. Identify every element that adds cognitive load. Classify as intrinsic, extraneous, or germane. Suggest specific simplifications."
Progressive Disclosure
Ideation
Show users only what they need at each step. Reveal complexity only as needed. AI can help you restructure information architectures using progressive disclosure principles.
→ "Here's all the information we show on [SCREEN]: [LIST]. Redesign the hierarchy using progressive disclosure. What should be shown immediately vs. on-demand vs. in settings?"
Error Prevention
Testing
Nielsen's heuristic #5 — design systems to prevent errors before they occur. AI can simulate failure modes and generate edge case scenarios to design for.
→ "You are a destructive user trying to break [FEATURE]. List 15 ways you might accidentally make an error, misunderstand a prompt, or reach an unintended state. Don't filter for likelihood."
Affinity Mapping
Synthesis
Clustering qualitative data into themes. After user research, affinity maps reveal patterns. AI can do rapid first-pass clustering of interview data to accelerate synthesis.
→ "Here are 40 raw observations from user interviews: [PASTE]. Group them into 5–8 themes. Name each theme with a user-centric statement (not a noun, but a need or behavior). List observations under each."
Emotional Design
Ideation
Don Norman's three levels: visceral (appearance), behavioral (usability), and reflective (meaning). AI helps map emotional journeys and design for peak emotional moments.
→ "Map the emotional arc of a user going through [FLOW]. Identify the peak positive and peak negative moments. For each negative moment, suggest one design intervention at the visceral, behavioral, and reflective level."
Heuristic Evaluation
Testing
Evaluating a UI against established usability principles. AI can rapidly apply Nielsen's 10 heuristics (or others) to design descriptions or transcripts.
→ "Evaluate this interface description against Nielsen's 10 Usability Heuristics: [DESCRIPTION]. For each heuristic, rate it 0–4 (0=not applicable, 1=no problem, 4=catastrophic) and explain why."
Prototype Workflow

A step-by-step AI-assisted process from brief to testable prototype — with the specific prompts that unlock each phase.

"The best prompt isn't the one that generates a design — it's the one that generates the right question to ask your users."
01
Frame the Problem
Before any design, crystallize the problem. AI helps you stress-test your problem statement and reframe it from the user's perspective.
"Rewrite this problem statement from a user perspective: [YOUR STATEMENT]. Then give me 3 alternative framings that might reveal different solution spaces."
02
Simulate a User
Have AI roleplay as your target persona to stress-test assumptions early — before investing in design.
"You are [PERSONA]. I'll describe a product concept. After I describe it, respond in character: what's your first reaction, what confuses you, what would make you keep using it?"
03
Generate Flow Options
Get multiple competing user flow structures before committing to wireframes. Compare them systematically.
"Give me 3 different user flow structures for [TASK]. For each: outline the steps, name the key UX tradeoff, and identify who it works best for."
04
Write Screen Copy First
UX writing shapes UX thinking. Draft all copy before visual design to avoid "lorem ipsum" decisions that define the real experience.
"Write all the copy for [SCREEN]: headline, body, CTA, helper text, error states, empty states. Voice: [ADJECTIVES]. Each element must serve a specific user need."
05
Spec the Prototype
Turn a concept into a Figma/Framer/code prototype brief. AI generates the interaction spec your prototype must cover.
"Write a low-fidelity prototype brief for [CONCEPT]. List: screens to build, key interactions to demonstrate, what should and shouldn't work, and what you want to learn from testing it."
06
Generate Test Tasks
Every prototype needs a test plan. AI generates realistic, unbiased task scenarios from your prototype spec.
"Write 4 usability test tasks for this prototype: [DESCRIBE PROTOTYPE]. Each task must: use scenario framing (not UI instructions), have a clear success criterion, and avoid leading language."
What AI Can't Replace

AI is your fastest first-draft collaborator — but it doesn't replace talking to real users. It doesn't replace the insight of watching someone struggle with your prototype for 30 seconds. It doesn't replace the judgment call you make when research says one thing and business says another. Use it to move faster and think wider — not to skip the human parts.

Prompt Patterns Copy these structures

Every great UX prompt follows a pattern. These are the building blocks.

Role + Context + Constraint
You are a [ROLE]. [CONTEXT ABOUT PRODUCT/USER]. [TASK]. Constraint: [LIMITATION OR FORMAT].
Simulate + React
Act as [PERSONA]. I'll describe [THING]. After I describe it, respond in-character with: [SPECIFIC REACTIONS TO GIVE].
Compare + Evaluate
Give me [N] different approaches to [PROBLEM]. For each: [ELEMENT 1], [ELEMENT 2], [ELEMENT 3]. Then recommend the best for [SPECIFIC CONTEXT].
Audit + Fix
Here is [ARTIFACT]: [CONTENT]. Evaluate it against [FRAMEWORK/CRITERIA]. For each issue: severity (1–3), why it's a problem, and a specific fix.
